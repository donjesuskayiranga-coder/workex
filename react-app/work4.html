Electric Field Due to
Continuous Charge Distribution

Understanding Integration in Electrostatics

1

Introduction

An electric field describes how electric charges influence the space around them.

When charge is concentrated at a point, we can easily calculate the electric field.

However: In many real situations, charge is spread continuously over an object. This is known as a continuous charge distribution.

2

What is a Continuous Charge Distribution?

Definition: A continuous charge distribution occurs when electric charge is spread uniformly along a line, over a surface, or throughout a volume, instead of being concentrated at a single point.

Linear

Charged rod/wire

Surface

Charged sheet

Volume

Charged sphere

3

Basic Method - Integration

To find the electric field due to a continuous charge distribution, we:

  • Divide the charge into very small elements dq
  • Each dq produces a small electric field dE
  • Total field = vector sum using integration
dE = k(dq/r²) r̂
E = k ∫ (dq/r²) r̂
4

Charge Density - What & Why?

What is Charge Density?

Charge density tells us how charge is distributed in space.

Why is this VERY important?

• We cannot integrate Q directly
• We must integrate dq
• Charge density allows us to express dq in terms of dx, dA, or dV

5

Types of Charge Density

Type Symbol Formula Unit dq =
Linear λ Q/L C/m λ dx
Surface σ Q/A C/m² σ dA
Volume ρ Q/V C/m³ ρ dV
6

General Formula

The total electric field at a point is obtained by integrating contributions from all charge elements.

E = k ∫ (dq/r²) r̂

Where:

  • k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²
  • dq = charge element (using density)
  • r = distance from dq to point
7

Charged Rod: Point on Extension

Point lies along the axis of the rod, distance 'a' from one end.

Key: All field contributions in same direction → direct addition

E = kQ / [a(a + L)]

Far away (a >> L): Rod behaves like point charge

8

Charged Rod: Perpendicular Bisector

Point on perpendicular bisector, distance 'a' from center.

Symmetry: Horizontal components cancel, only vertical remains!

E = kQa / √[a² + (L/2)²]
9

Infinite Charged Rod

For an infinitely long rod, field depends only on distance 'a'.

E = 2kλ / a

Observations:

  • Depends on λ (not total Q)
  • Decreases as 1/a (not 1/a²)
  • Perpendicular to rod
10

Example: Half +Q, Half −Q Rod

One half positive, other half negative.

On perpendicular bisector: Fields from both halves point same direction and add!

Far away: Net field → zero (total charge is zero)

11

Infinite Sheet of Charge

Produces uniform field independent of distance!

E = σ / (2ε₀)

Where: ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²/(N·m²)

Field perpendicular to sheet, constant magnitude everywhere!

12

Problem 1: Linear Charge Density

Problem: Rod of length 2 m, charge 8 μC. Find λ.

Given: Q = 8 × 10⁻⁶ C, L = 2 m
Formula: λ = Q/L
Calculate: λ = 8×10⁻⁶/2 = 4×10⁻⁶ C/m
Answer: λ = 4 μC/m
13

Problem 2: Surface Charge Density

Problem: Square sheet, area 0.5 m², charge 10 μC. Find σ.

Given: Q = 10 × 10⁻⁶ C, A = 0.5 m²
Formula: σ = Q/A
Calculate: σ = 10×10⁻⁶/0.5 = 2×10⁻⁵ C/m²
Answer: σ = 2×10⁻⁵ C/m²
14

Problem 3: Field on Rod Axis

Problem: Rod L=1m, Q=5μC. Point 0.5m from end. Find E.

Given: Q=5×10⁻⁶C, L=1m, a=0.5m, k=9×10⁹
Formula: E = kQ/[a(a+L)]
Calc: E = 9×10⁹×5×10⁻⁶/[0.5×1.5]
E = 45×10³/0.75
Answer: E = 6.0×10⁴ N/C
15

Problem 4: Perpendicular Bisector

Problem: Rod L=2m, Q=4μC. Point 1m from center. Find E.

Given: Q=4×10⁻⁶C, L=2m, a=1m
Formula: E = kQa/√[a²+(L/2)²]
Calc: E = 9×10⁹×4×10⁻⁶×1/√2
E = 36×10³/1.414
Answer: E ≈ 2.55×10⁴ N/C
16

Problem 5: Infinite Rod

Problem: Infinite wire, λ=3μC/m. Distance 0.2m. Find E.

Given: λ=3×10⁻⁶ C/m, a=0.2m
Formula: E = 2kλ/a
Calc: E = 2×9×10⁹×3×10⁻⁶/0.2
E = 54×10³/0.2
Answer: E = 2.7×10⁵ N/C
17

Problem 6: Infinite Sheet

Problem: Sheet σ=6×10⁻⁶ C/m². Find E.

Given: σ=6×10⁻⁶ C/m²
Formula: E = σ/(2ε₀)
Calc: E = 6×10⁻⁶/(2×8.85×10⁻¹²)
E = 6×10⁻⁶/1.77×10⁻¹¹
Answer: E ≈ 3.39×10⁵ N/C
18

Key Physical Meanings

1. Why Integration?
Charge is continuous, cannot be treated as single point

2. Symmetry
Simplifies calculations by canceling components

3. Far-Field Behavior
At large distances, acts like point charge

19

Questions for Classmates

Q1: Why do we use integration?

Q2: Why do horizontal components cancel in perpendicular bisector?

Q3: Which density has unit C/m²?

Q4: Why is infinite sheet field constant?

Q5: When does rod behave like point charge?

20

Answers

A1: Charge is continuous, not a single point

A2: Symmetry—equal charges opposite sides create equal/opposite horizontal fields

A3: Surface charge density (σ)

A4: Far charge contributions compensate—no distance dependence

A5: When observation point very far (distance >> length)

21

Conclusion

The electric field due to continuous charge distribution is found by:

  • Dividing charge into small elements (dq)
  • Applying Coulomb's law (dq → dE)
  • Integrating over entire distribution
  • Using charge density (λ, σ, ρ) to express dq
  • Applying symmetry to simplify

Integration transforms discrete into continuous!
